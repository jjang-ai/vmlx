Great question! Both **KV cache** and **prefix cache** are used in the context of **large language models (LLMs)**, particularly during **autoregressive generation** (like in text generation), to improve efficiency by avoiding redundant computations. However, they serve different purposes and are used in different ways.

Let’s break down the differences:

---

### 🔹 1. KV Cache (Key-Value Cache)

**What it is:**
- A **KV cache** stores the **key (K)** and **value (V)** tensors of the model’s self-attention layers for previously generated tokens.
- It is used to **avoid recomputing attention over already generated tokens** when generating the next token.

**Why it's used:**
- In autoregressive generation, each new token depends on all previous tokens.
- During attention computation, the model computes attention between the current token and all previous tokens.
- If you recompute attention for every token, you’d be doing redundant work.
- The KV cache stores the keys and values from the previous tokens, so when generating the next token, the model can **look up** the stored K and V instead of re-computing them.

**Structure:**
- For each position `t`, you store:
  - `K_t` (key matrix)
  - `V_t` (value matrix)
- These are used in the attention computation for the next token.

**Use case:**
- Standard in all modern LLMs (e.g., LLaMA, GPT, Mistral).
- Essential for **real-time, efficient generation** (e.g., chatbots, text completion).

**Efficiency gain:**
- Reduces computational cost from O(n²) to O(1) per new token (in terms of attention computation).

---

### 🔹 2. Prefix Cache

**What it is:**
- A **prefix cache** is a **more general concept** that can refer to storing **the entire sequence of tokens (or their representations)** up to a certain point — often used in **beam search**, **multi-turn conversations**, or **long-context reasoning**.
- It may include **token IDs**, **embeddings**, or **attention states**.
- It's not strictly limited to keys and values — it can store more than just K and V.
